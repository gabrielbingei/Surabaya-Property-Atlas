The historical spine, in four moves

  • 1743–1900 — the river mouth. Everything begins at the Kalimas estuary. The Dutch wijkenstelsel (from 1843) hard-zones the city by race: European quarter west of the Red Bridge, Chinatown immediately east of it, the Arab quarter around Sunan Ampel's mosque to the north, Javanese and Madurese kampung filling the gaps. Chinese residents were legally barred from living or working outside their quarter until 1918. Kota Lama's four quarters — European, Chinese, Arab, Malay — are a legible fossil of this, and were restored and reopened as a tourist precinct in July 2024.
  • 1900–1942 — the southward garden suburb. The Dutch move their own elite out of the fetid port. Ketabang is master-planned by Cosman Citroen from 1916; the Darmo villa district appears in the same period. The city's prestige axis rotates from the river to Jalan Raya Darmo. This is the last time the state decides where the rich live.
  • 1970s–1980s — the first suburbs. Darmo Permai, Darmo Satelit, Kupang Indah west; Dharmahusada Indah, Manyar and Galaxy east; Perumnas blocks at Manukan and Tandes. Private developers take over, and the sorting logic becomes "which estate did you buy into."
  • 1990s–now — the western land grab, then the eastern fill-in. Between 1990 and 1995 three groups carve up the western rice fields: Graha Famili (280 ha, 1991), Bukit Darmo Golf (126 ha, 1991), Pakuwon Indah (400 ha, 1993) and Ciputra's CitraRaya/CitraLand (a 2,000–2,500 ha masterplan, 1993). Ciputra opens the golf course in 1995 and builds a city around it. The east answers with Pakuwon City on the old Laguna Indah tambak. Surabaya's elite geography today is essentially the map those four permits drew.

Reconcile these before you buy anything

A central bank index near zero and brokers quoting double-digits are not both describing the same asset. What is actually happening: primary (developer) prices are administered upward — Ciputra and Pakuwon control release volumes and raise list prices each phase — while secondary asking prices are sticky and transacting below ask. Capital gain in Surabaya is a land story concentrated on newly-opened frontier (JLLB in the west, OERR in the east), not a building story in mature estates. And note that Rp 10 M+ landed product has a thin buyer pool: several listings above are visibly cut by Rp 5 M and still sitting.

Segment read

  • Landed > vertical, still. Surabaya has never been a strong apartment market — absorption is slow and capital gain thin. Apartments make sense here as rental yield plays near campuses and along MERR, not as appreciation plays.
  • Rental yield inverts the price map. The east and south student belts (Keputih, Gebang, Sukolilo, Siwalankerto, Ketintang) and the industrial fringes (Rungkut/SIER) throw off the best percentage yields, because the entry price is low and the tenant pool is bottomless. The west's Rp 10 M houses are capital-preservation assets with weak yields, partly let to a small expat pool.
  • Ruko is the quiet Surabaya trade. Trading-city DNA: shophouses generate cash flow and are the default asset of the Chinese-Indonesian business class. G-Walk, District 9, One Arcade, San Antonio.
  • Liquidity asymmetry. West resells fast because the buyer pool is broad and pre-formed. East appreciates faster off a lower base but takes longer to exit. That is the actual trade-off, stripped of the marketing.

Where Bukit Telaga Golf actually sits

Short answer: top tier, but not the top of the city. It is a premium golf-and-lake cluster inside CitraLand, in Sambikerep–Lakarsantri, West Surabaya — Tier 1 within a Tier 1 township, which is a different thing from being Surabaya's single most expensive address.

The case for it

  • Frontage. Golf and lake views are the scarce good inside a 2,500 ha masterplan. Ciputra Golf — 100 ha, 27 holes, open since November 1995, East Java's largest and the city's only night golf — is 2–4 minutes away, and BTG plots sit on it.
  • Infrastructure that the rest of the city doesn't have. CitraLand runs underground utilities and its own water treatment plant. In a city with Surabaya's water and drainage record, that is a real, not cosmetic, premium.
  • The school-and-hospital lock-in. Sekolah Ciputra (IB), Cita Hati West, Surabaya Intercultural School, Universitas Ciputra; National Hospital and Ciputra Hospital. For the buyer profile this estate is aimed at, these are not amenities — they are the reason for the purchase.
  • Community density. CitraLand houses roughly 13,000 households / ~44,000 residents across ~1,100 ha built of the 2,500 ha plan, and about 12.5% of its stock is above Rp 10 M. There is a critical mass of peers, which sustains resale.
  • The infrastructure catalyst. The Jalur Lingkar Luar Barat (JLLB) and the Radial Road run through CitraLand's flank and are the single largest identified upside driver for west-side land.

The case against it

  • The developer is also your competitor. Ciputra still has ~1,400 ha unbuilt and keeps launching golf-adjacent clusters (Bukit Golf Internasional, Crystal Golf, Woodland, Raffles Garden, South Emerald Mansion). Every new phase competes with your resale. This is the structural cap on secondary appreciation inside any large Ciputra estate.
  • Title. Many BTG listings are PPJB or HGB, not SHM. That is normal in a developer township and normally fine, but it is not the same asset as a freehold Darmo villa, and it prices differently.
  • Thin pool above Rp 10 M. A Rp 22 M house in Surabaya has a buyer list you could write on one page.
  • Prestige is contested. Ask an old Surabaya family and the answer is Darmo. Ask a 2015-vintage entrepreneur and it's CitraLand. Ask someone who wants to be unseen and it's Graha Famili. BTG wins the third-place argument convincingly and does not win the first two.

Verdict

BTG is the strongest lifestyle-and-infrastructure proposition in Surabaya's Rp 9–22 M band, and a weaker capital-gain proposition than an equivalent sum deployed on frontier land along the JLLB or OERR. Buy it to live in it. If you want the appreciation, the money goes to Wiyung/Kebraon in the west or Gunung Anyar/Medokan in the east — and you accept a worse life for a better number.

Who lives where, and who is arriving

Surabaya's residential map is not only a price map. It is also an ethnic and religious map, and has been since 1843 — the difference is that the sorting is now voluntary and runs on schools rather than on colonial statute.

The single most important sorting fact

Surabaya's Chinese-Indonesian community was legally confined to the Pecinan east of the Red Bridge until 1918. Over the following century it moved — first to the centre, then decisively west and south. Today the western elite estates and their entire supporting institutional apparatus (Petra, Cita Hati, Sekolah Ciputra, SIS, Universitas Ciputra, National Hospital, the GBI and Catholic parishes inside CitraLand) exist substantially because of that community's demand. The 12.8% Christian minority is disproportionately located in the same estates. If you want to understand why Surabaya's money went west rather than staying in Darmo, this — not the golf course — is the causal thread. The golf course is what the demand bought.

Where in-migrants go, and why

Students
→ Keputih, Gebang, Sukolilo, Klampis, Siwalankerto, Ketintang

ITS, Unair, UNESA, Petra, Ubaya, UWK, Universitas Ciputra. The kost economy. Highest rental yields in the city, lowest capital gain. Turns over every four years and never empties.

Madurese in-migrants
→ Kenjeran, Semampir, Bulak, Sidotopo

Kin networks, fishing, scrap and loak trade, the warung Madura franchise. Arrives via Suramadu, settles where family already is. This is chain migration, not price-shopping.

Industrial & informal labour
→ Rungkut/SIER fringe, Margomulyo, Tandes, Krembangan, Karangpilang

Follows the estates: SIER in the east, the port-and-warehouse belt in the north-west. Housed in contract-kost and dense kampung; historically the source of the riverbank and rail-margin settlements the city has been clearing into rusunawa.

Young middle-class families
→ Wiyung, Kebraon, Bukit Palma, Gunung Anyar, and across the border

Priced out of everything inside the ring. Rp 900 jt–2 M product, KPR instalments in the Rp 4–6 jt range. This cohort is the reason satellite imagery shows the sharpest 2010–2020 land-use change at the Sidoarjo and Gresik borders, not inside the city.

Professionals & medics
→ Dharmahusada Indah, Manyar, Galaxy, Mulyosari

Proximity to Unair, RSUD Dr. Soetomo, ITS. The most stable, least speculative housing sub-market in Surabaya.

Business families & the wealthy
→ CitraLand, Graha Famili, Pakuwon Indah, Pakuwon City

Schools, hospitals, security, community, and the ability to build a 500–1,200 m² house — which is impossible on a colonial plot in Darmo without a heritage fight. See above.

Expatriates
→ Pakuwon Indah, Graha Famili, CitraLand, Sungkono apartments

A small pool by Jakarta standards, tied to SIS and the international schools. Matters more for rental support in the west than for total demand.

Outbound: the city itself
→ Sidoarjo, Gresik

Surabaya's own population is close to flat. The metro (Gerbangkertosusila) is where the growth is. Pondok Tjandra, Delta Sari, Rewwin, Buduran, Driyorejo, Menganti — the commuter belt is where the next million people will actually live.

The historical scale of this: as of 2015, roughly 1.04 million lifetime in-migrants — about 37% of the city's population — had moved into Surabaya. The city has an unusually direct policy grip on it (residency checks, targeted relocation into rusunawa, sticker-based poverty registration), which is why its slum footprint has shrunk in a way Jakarta's has not, even as its poverty is still concentrated in the same kecamatan it was in 2004: Tambaksari, Sawahan, Wonokromo, Semampir, Tegalsari.

2030–2045: where the next map gets drawn

Three infrastructure lines and one very contested pile of sand.

1. JLLB + Radial Road — the western frontier

The Jalur Lingkar Luar Barat and the Radial Road toward HR Muhammad are what turn CitraLand's remaining ~1,400 unbuilt hectares, plus Benowo, Pakal, Sememi and Tandes, from periphery into product. CitraLand is explicit that it will extend west and north from its current footprint. This is the highest-confidence forecast on this page: the west keeps going, and the entry-level west (Kebraon, Harvest Link, Bukit Palma, Taman Puspa Raya) is where the percentage gains are.

2. OERR / JLLT — the eastern frontier

The eastern outer ring road opens Gunung Anyar, Medokan Ayu, Rungkut Menanggal and the southern Sukolilo tambak. It is the reason Pakuwon City's own marketing has been promising an "eastern ring road" uplift for years. Cheaper base, higher percentage upside, thinner exit — and a flood-risk profile the DPRD has publicly warned about.

3. Verticalisation of MERR and A. Yani

Where the city grows without spreading. Apartment product along Ir. Soekarno (MERR) and the A. Yani frontage — Klaska Residence is the archetype. This is a rental-yield market attached to campuses and offices, and it should be underwritten as one.

4. Surabaya Waterfront Land — the wildcard

Read this before you price anything east of Kenjeran

SWL is a 1,084–1,184 ha reclamation of four artificial islands off the Kenjeran–Pamurbaya coast, promoted by PT Granting Jaya (the Kenjeran Park group), valued around Rp 72 trillion, granted National Strategic Project (PSN) status in April 2024, and programmed over roughly 20 years — 15 of them just for reclamation and land maturation. The blocks include a maritime zone, a fisheries SEZ, and — the part that matters for this atlas — villa estate, apartments and ruko.

And it is legally unresolved. It sits in coastal zones that Perda Kota Surabaya 12/2014 (RTRW 2014–2034) protects as mangrove and that East Java's Perda 10/2024 designates for ecosystem management, where reclamation is prohibited. The proponent went to the coordinating ministry precisely because the project does not conform to provincial spatial plan. The mayor has publicly questioned it; the previous mayor opposes it; fishing communities across twelve kelurahan from Bulak to Gunung Anyar Tambak have organised against it; ITS analysts have called the PSN designation premature and noted PSN status can be revoked. As of 2026 the marine affairs ministry is still evaluating its marine spatial conformity.

How to hold this: as a live option, not a plan. If SWL proceeds, the east coast is re-rated and Pakuwon City/Kenjeran becomes waterfront-adjacent rather than edge-of-city. If it is cancelled or stalls — the base case on a 20-year build with this much legal friction — nothing changes, except that some people will have paid frontier prices for land whose story depended on it. Do not underwrite east-coast land on the assumption SWL happens.

The risk register, honestly

  • The index is flat. +0.21% q/q and a −12.5% demand-supply ratio are not the numbers of a hot market. Surabaya in mid-2026 is a buyer's market with a seller's narrative.
  • Luxury landed oversupply in the west. Ciputra's land bank is the supply curve. It caps your resale.
  • Flood and rob. North and east coast, and any drainage downstream of a reclamation.
  • Title. PPJB / HGB is the norm in townships. Price it accordingly; don't compare it to SHM.
  • Concentration. Two developers — Ciputra and Pakuwon — set the price of prestige in this city. That is a governance risk as much as a market one.
